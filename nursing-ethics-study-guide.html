What is INC?

Indian Nursing Council — The Apex Body

The Indian Nursing Council (INC) is a statutory body established under the Indian Nursing Council Act, 1947. It is the apex regulatory body that governs nursing education and practice across India.

Key Functions of INC

  • Prescribes standards for nursing education
  • Registers nurses at national level
  • Maintains uniform standards of nursing practice
  • Lays down Code of Ethics and Code of Professional Conduct
  • Recognizes nursing institutions across India
Key Fact INC was constituted in 1949 under the Indian Nursing Council Act 1947. Headquarters: New Delhi.
Two Key Documents
01

Code of Ethics

A set of moral principles that guide nurses in making ethical decisions and maintaining standards of moral behavior in professional practice.

02

Code of Professional Conduct

Specific rules and behavioral standards that define what is expected of registered nurses in their professional role and interactions.

⚠ Exam Distinction

Code of Ethics = MORAL principles (values/beliefs). Code of Conduct = BEHAVIORAL rules (what to do/not do). Both are published by INC and ICN.

UNIT Code of Ethics — Full Coverage

Moral framework governing nursing decisions

The Code of Ethics for Nurses (ICN, revised 2021; adapted by INC) defines the moral obligations of nurses. It is built on four fundamental elements.

Element 1: Nurses & People

Primary responsibility is to the patient/community

  • Nurse's primary professional responsibility is to people requiring nursing care
  • Promotes an environment where human rights, values, customs and spiritual beliefs are respected
  • Ensures patient receives sufficient information to give informed consent
  • Maintains confidentiality of personal information
  • Shares information appropriately — only with those professionally involved in care
  • Protects the patient's right to privacy
  • Nurse should be sensitive to cultural differences
  • Advocates for equity and social justice in resource distribution
Core Principle The nurse's loyalty is FIRST to the patient — not the institution, not the physician, not the government.
Element 2: Nurses & Practice

Accountability for personal nursing practice

  • Accountable for own nursing practice and maintaining competence
  • Maintains standards of personal health to avoid compromising patient care
  • Uses judgment in delegating work to others — remains accountable
  • Maintains dignity and privacy during care
  • Promotes a culture of safety — reports unsafe conditions
  • Adheres to safe practice even in staff shortage situations
  • Does NOT engage in practices that are deceptive, dishonest, or harmful
  • Maintains nursing competence through continuous professional development
⚠ High Yield

If a nurse delegates a task to an unqualified person and harm occurs — the NURSE remains accountable. Delegation does not transfer accountability.

Element 3: Nurses & the Profession

Advancing the nursing profession

  • Plays a major role in defining and maintaining acceptable standards of practice
  • Actively involved in developing nursing knowledge through research
  • Participates in developing and sustaining a core of professional values
  • Through professional association, nurses participate in creating a positive work environment
  • Upholds integrity of the nursing profession
  • Contributes to evidence-based practice
  • Mentors students and other nurses (professional responsibility)
Element 4: Nurses & Co-workers

Collaborative and professional relationships

  • Maintains collaborative and respectful relationships with co-workers in nursing and other fields
  • Takes action to safeguard individuals when care is endangered by a co-worker or any other person
  • Nurses working in this context should have safeguards in place to protect themselves for reporting unsafe care
  • Takes appropriate action if another health worker's conduct is incompetent, unethical, or illegal
  • Supports and guides student nurses (mentorship)
Key Concept If a co-worker is impaired (alcohol/drugs) or acting unsafely — the nurse has an ETHICAL DUTY to report, not ignore.

Code of Professional Conduct — Introduction

Rules that govern nursing behavior in practice

The Code of Professional Conduct specifies behavioral standards that all registered nurses must follow. It protects the public, guides the nurse, and maintains trust in the profession.

Definition Professional conduct = behavior expected of a nurse in their professional capacity, across all settings of practice.
Core Duties Under Professional Conduct

1. Responsibility to Patients

  • Provide safe, competent, and compassionate care at all times
  • Respect the patient's dignity, privacy, and autonomy
  • Obtain informed consent before any procedure
  • Maintain confidentiality — do not disclose patient information without consent
  • Act as patient advocate — protect patient from harm
  • Report any adverse events or near misses honestly
  • Never abandon a patient in need of care (non-abandonment)

2. Responsibility to Self

  • Maintain personal competence and continued education
  • Do not practice while impaired (illness, fatigue, substances)
  • Maintain own physical and mental health
  • Recognize personal and professional limits — seek help when needed
3. Responsibility to the Profession
  • Uphold the reputation and integrity of the nursing profession
  • Maintain valid registration with state nursing council
  • Cooperate with professional body investigations
  • Not engage in conduct that would bring the profession into disrepute
  • Contribute to professional development of colleagues
  • Participate in research and evidence-based practice

4. Responsibility to Society

  • Promote public health and community welfare
  • Participate in health education and community programs
  • Support national health programs (immunization, RNTCP, etc.)
  • Advocate for social justice and equitable healthcare
  • Report notifiable diseases as per legal requirement

Documentation & Records
  • Accurate, complete, and timely documentation is a professional duty
  • Never alter or falsify records — legal and ethical violation
  • Countersigning: supervisor countersigns student nurse notes
  • Medical records are legal documents — admissible in court
  • Patient has the right to access their own records
  • Follow hospital policy on electronic health records (EHR) confidentiality
⚠ Exam Focus

Falsifying nursing records = misconduct + legal liability. "If it's not documented, it's not done" — standard nursing principle.

The 8 Core Ethical Principles

Must-know for every nursing ethics exam

🧠 Memory Aid — ABCDEFGJ
Autonomy · Beneficence · Confidentiality · Do No Harm · Equality · Fidelity · Good Faith · Justice
1. Autonomy

The patient's right to make their own decisions about their healthcare, free from coercion.

  • Basis of informed consent
  • Patient can refuse treatment — nurse must respect this
  • Requires nurse to provide complete, accurate information
  • Nurse must document patient's decision even if nurse disagrees
  • Limited when patient lacks decision-making capacity (unconscious, minors)
Clinical Example Patient refuses blood transfusion on religious grounds (Jehovah's Witness). Nurse must respect autonomy and document refusal, even if disagreeing.
2. Beneficence

The duty to do good — act in the best interest of the patient.

  • Every nursing action should benefit the patient
  • Active promotion of patient's wellbeing
  • Goes beyond just avoiding harm — requires positive action
  • Basis of therapeutic nursing care

3. Non-Maleficence

The duty to do no harm — "Primum non nocere" (Latin: First, do no harm).

  • Avoid causing physical, emotional, or financial harm
  • Risk vs. benefit analysis before procedures
  • Reporting medication errors protects patient (non-maleficence)
  • Sometimes conflicts with beneficence (painful treatment for long-term benefit)
⚠ Common Exam Trick

Beneficence = do GOOD. Non-maleficence = avoid HARM. They are different. A nurse giving a painful injection (harm) to cure disease (good) = conflict between the two, resolved by weighing net benefit.

4. Justice

Fair, equitable treatment for all patients. Resources are distributed without discrimination.

  • Equal care regardless of race, religion, gender, socioeconomic status, caste
  • Basis of triage (prioritizing by need, not by status)
  • Distributive justice: fair allocation of healthcare resources
  • Procedural justice: fair process in decision making
Clinical Example In ICU with one ventilator and two patients — deciding who gets it based on clinical need (not wealth) = Justice principle.
5. Fidelity (Faithfulness)

Keeping promises and commitments to patients and colleagues.

  • Following through on commitments made to patients
  • Maintaining confidentiality (keeping the trust promise)
  • Not abandoning patients mid-care
  • Basis of therapeutic nurse-patient relationship

6. Veracity (Truthfulness)

Obligation to tell the truth to patients and colleagues.

  • Truth-telling even when difficult (bad diagnosis)
  • Basis of informed consent
  • Deception is an ethical violation
  • Conflict: sometimes truth may harm (→ therapeutic privilege, rarely used)
7. Confidentiality

Patient information shared in professional context must not be disclosed without consent.

  • Protected by both ethics AND law
  • Nurse can share information only with care team involved in patient's treatment
  • Exceptions (mandatory disclosure):
    • Notifiable diseases (TB, cholera, plague, COVID-19)
    • Court order / legal requirement
    • Danger to self or others
    • Child abuse
  • Social media posts about patients = SEVERE ethical violation

8. Accountability

Being answerable for one's own nursing actions and decisions.

  • Cannot be delegated — even if action delegated, accountability remains
  • Nurse is accountable to: patient, profession, employer, law, self
  • Requires honest error reporting
Ethical Conflicts & Decision-Making

Steps in Ethical Decision Making

  1. Identify the ethical problem/dilemma
  2. Gather all relevant information (medical, legal, social)
  3. Identify all people involved (stakeholders)
  4. List possible courses of action
  5. Apply ethical principles to each option
  6. Make a decision — implement and evaluate
  7. Document the process
Common Ethical Dilemmas in Nursing Euthanasia · Abortion · Organ harvesting · Resuscitation decisions · Truth-telling in terminal illness · Resource allocation · Research on vulnerable groups
Professional Misconduct — What It Is

Behavior that falls below the standard expected of a registered nurse. Can lead to suspension or removal from nursing register.

Examples of Misconduct

  • Clinical: Medication errors due to negligence, wrong patient, wrong dose
  • Records: Falsifying, altering, or destroying patient records
  • Boundaries: Sexual relationship with patient
  • Substance: Practicing while under influence of drugs or alcohol
  • Fraud: Claiming qualifications not held; obtaining registration fraudulently
  • Confidentiality: Unauthorized disclosure of patient information
  • Abandonment: Leaving a patient without appropriate handover
  • Criminal: Conviction for a crime (theft, assault)
⚠ Exam Focus

Misconduct is judged by what a "reasonable nurse" would have done in the same circumstances. Standard = not perfection, but reasonable competence.

Negligence in Nursing

All 4 elements must be present to prove negligence. Missing any one = not legally negligent (may still be ethical violation).

Types of Negligence

  • Civil Negligence — Patient sues for compensation
  • Criminal Negligence — Gross neglect; IPC Section 304A applies
  • Professional Negligence (Malpractice) — Below standard by professional
Informed Consent — Detailed
  • Must be voluntary (no coercion or undue influence)
  • Patient must be competent (legally and mentally)
  • Must be informed — full information about procedure, risks, alternatives
  • Written consent for invasive procedures, surgeries, research
  • Verbal consent for routine care (BP check, IV insertion)

Who Can Give Consent?

  • Adult (≥18 yrs) with decision-making capacity — gives own consent
  • Minor (below 18) — parent or legal guardian
  • Unconscious/incapacitated — next of kin / legal guardian
  • Emergency — implied consent (assumed patient would consent to life-saving care)
Special Cases Mentally ill patients (Mental Health Act 2017): if voluntarily admitted, they consent. If involuntarily admitted, nominated representative or family gives consent.
Patient Rights (Bill of Rights)
  • Right to information about their condition and treatment
  • Right to informed consent and to refuse treatment
  • Right to confidentiality and privacy
  • Right to dignified and respectful care
  • Right to second opinion
  • Right to access medical records
  • Right to complain and seek redress
  • Right to emergency care (even without advance payment)
⚠ Important

The Supreme Court of India has ruled that the right to health is part of the right to life under Article 21 of the Constitution.

Whistleblowing in Nursing

Reporting unsafe practices or misconduct by a colleague, superior, or institution.

  • Ethically REQUIRED if patient safety is at risk
  • Nurse has a duty to report — not just an option
  • Internal reporting first (charge nurse → nursing officer → administration)
  • External reporting if internal fails (State Nursing Council, regulatory body)
  • Nurse is protected from retaliation under professional ethics (though Indian laws are weaker on this vs. Western countries)
